KUBRICK

STANLEY KUBRICK

This exhibition invites visitors to step into the cinematic universe of Stanley Kubrick, offering a unique exploration of his groundbreaking vision and storytelling. Designed as an immersive experience, the exhibition combines visual installations, film excerpts, and curated spaces that reflect the atmosphere of his most influential works. Visitors are guided through different stages of Kubrick’s career, discovering how his ideas evolved and how he redefined filmmaking through innovation and precision.

ABOUT US

The Film Vault Museum opens its 45th annual exhibition with a filmmaker who needs no introduction — and yet, somehow, always rewards a closer look. Since we first opened our doors in 1961, we have chosen, year after year, a single figure or film that has shaped the course of cinema. This year, the choice was never in question.

AT THIS MOMENTS

Stanley Kubrick (New York, 1928 – Hertfordshire, 1999) made barely thirteen feature films. Thirteen. And yet the body of work he left behind has proven virtually inexhaustible. The existential menace of Killer's Kiss, the cold institutional horror of Paths of Glory, the pitch-black comedy of Dr. Strangelove, the psychological dread of The Shining, the vast, unsettling silence of 2001 — these are not simply great films. They are arguments about what cinema can be: what it can do to time, to space, to the mind of the person sitting in the dark.

The exhibition brings together more than 200 original items: scripts covered in his own handwriting, never-before-seen storyboards, personal correspondence with actors and producers, costumes, props, and selections from his private archives — much of it on public display in Spain for the very first time.

This is a space built on a single belief, held for over six decades: that cinema deserves the same attention, care, and reverence we give to any great art form. Every year we pick a new subject. Every year the building becomes something else entirely. This year, it becomes Kubrick's world.

The main changes: shorter, punchier sentences where it counts, a more conversational rhythm, and small additions like "Thirteen. And yet—" or "the person sitting in the dark" that feel written rather than translated.
